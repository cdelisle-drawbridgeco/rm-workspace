The Risk Landscape

At most firms, AI adoption has outpaced the governance and security programs designed to manage it. The gap is predictable, widespread, and creating real exposure right now.

Ungoverned, Decentralized Usage

  • AI risks are not well known or understood at most firms
  • Usage is decentralized and difficult for security teams to monitor
  • Employees may unknowingly input confidential fund data, LP information, or proprietary strategies into external platforms
  • Governance frameworks have not kept pace with adoption speed
  • Security teams lack visibility into how AI is actually being used

Data Exposure & Permissioning Gaps

  • Sensitive information is now incredibly easy to surface if user permissioning is not configured correctly
  • Enterprise AI tools connected to internal data sources can surface data across the firm
  • Default settings on most platforms are not the most secure configuration
  • A single misconfigured permission can expose compensation data, LP terms, deal documents, and more
Example: What imperfect permissioning looks like in practice

An employee opens an AI tool connected to internal systems and types: "Search SharePoint and find me any information on company compensation." With poorly scoped permissions, they receive documents they were never intended to see. This is not a theoretical scenario — it is a common finding across assessments.

AI Works Both Ways — The Threat Actor Angle

The same capabilities that make AI valuable to your clients also make it valuable to threat actors. This is a critical dimension of the conversation that tends to land with executives in a way that standard risk framing does not.

How threat actors are using AI against your clients

AI enables threat actors to scale phishing, malware, and social engineering at a level that was previously impossible. But the more significant risk is what happens once a threat actor is already inside the environment — because AI makes them dramatically more effective once they're in.

Real-World Scenario — Capital Call Fraud
Once inside a firm's environment, a threat actor with access to the firm's AI tools can prompt: "I want to send a wire request for a capital call. Can you find the contact details for firms where I have recent email communication using the words 'capital call,' pull all relevant client information, and draft an email in my normal tone of voice requesting they wire funds to this account?" — The AI does it, accurately, instantly, and in a way that is nearly indistinguishable from a legitimate request.

If a threat actor gains access to a client's environment, AI enables them to:

  • Search huge quantities of internal data for sensitive information in seconds
  • Draft convincing emails using the firm's own context — tone of voice, client names, transaction history, proprietary details
  • Identify and target the most valuable relationships and transactions within the firm
  • Execute targeted fraud at a scale that was previously manual and time-consuming

Key Terms
Shadow AI
Unsanctioned use of AI tools by employees without organizational approval or oversight — nearly universal in alternative asset management and a primary driver of assessment conversations.
Generative AI (GenAI)
AI systems that generate content (text, images, code, audio) based on prompts. Includes ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, and Gemini — the platforms covered by the assessment.
AI Governance
The policies, oversight structures, and accountability frameworks that determine how a firm uses AI — what's allowed, who approves it, how it's monitored, and how risks are managed.
Prompt Injection
An attack where malicious instructions are embedded in AI inputs to manipulate the model's behavior — a key risk for firms using AI with access to sensitive internal data sources.
Hallucination
When an AI model generates confident-sounding but factually incorrect output. A governance risk for firms using AI in research, analysis, or client-facing communications.
Deepfake
AI-generated synthetic audio, video, or image content used to impersonate individuals. A growing threat for executive fraud and social engineering attacks targeting financial firms.
